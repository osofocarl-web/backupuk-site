Welcome to Module 8 of the Level 4 Foundational Bible School Certificate Program.

The New Testament makes a sharp and uncomfortable distinction between "the crowds" and "the disciples." The crowds followed Jesus to see miracles and eat multiplied bread. The disciples followed Jesus to the cross. Jesus never called people to be mere believers; He distinctly commanded them to be disciples.

### 1. What is a Disciple?
In the first-century Greco-Roman world, a disciple (*mathetes* in Greek) was an apprentice. A disciple did not merely attend a weekly lecture by their master to learn interesting facts. An apprentice actually lived with the master, studied their every move, imitated their behavior, and structured their entire life around adopting the master's trade and philosophy.

*"Whoever claims to live in him must live as Jesus did."* (1 John 2:6)

A Christian disciple is an intentional apprentice of Jesus. Our goal is not simply intellectual agreement with theological doctrines; our goal is full behavioral and spiritual replication of our Master.

### 2. The Radical Terms of Apprenticeship
Jesus was never interested in gathering a massive, uncommitted crowd. On multiple occasions, when the crowds grew too large, Jesus would intentionally say something difficult to thin out the uncommitted. His terms for discipleship are shocking to modern, comfortable Christianity:

*"Then Jesus said to his disciples, 'Whoever wants to be my disciple must deny themselves and take up their cross and follow me. For whoever wants to save their life will lose it, but whoever loses their life for me will find it.'"* (Matthew 16:24-25)

**There are three unyielding conditions here:**
1.  **Denial of Self:** You no longer hold the deed to your own life. Your ambitions, your money, your sexuality, and your time are no longer yours to dictate. Jesus is Lord of all, or He is not Lord at all.
2.  **Taking up the Cross:** In the first century, the cross was not a piece of jewelry; it was an instrument of humiliating execution. To take up your cross means accepting full identification with Christ, even when it results in social rejection, persecution, or physical suffering. 
3.  **Following Him:** This is an active verb. Discipleship requires unrelenting obedience to His commands, day after day.

### 3. The Mathematics of Counting the Cost
Why would Jesus demand such a high price? Because He demands an un-divided heart.
He uses the analogy of a builder constructing a tower:
*"Suppose one of you wants to build a tower. Won’t you first sit down and estimate the cost to see if you have enough money to complete it?"* (Luke 14:28)

Discipleship is an "all-in" proposition. You cannot serve two masters (Matthew 6:24). If you attempt to follow Jesus while maintaining a grip on your worldly idols, you will ultimately fail at both. You must deliberately evaluate what following Jesus will cost you—relationships, career choices, comfort—and joyfully trade them for the surpassing worth of knowing Christ.

### 4. The Joy of Surrender
While the cost of discipleship is everything you have, the reward of discipleship is *everything He has*. When you lay down the small, pathetic kingdom of your own autonomy, you inherit the majestic, eternal Kingdom of Heaven.

*"But whatever were gains to me I now consider loss for the sake of Christ. What is more, I consider everything a loss because of the surpassing worth of knowing Christ Jesus my Lord, for whose sake I have lost all things."* (Philippians 3:7-8)

#### Practical Application for Today
Discipleship is intensely practical.
1.  **Identify Your Idols:** What is the one thing in your life you are terrified of God asking you to give up? That is your idol. Repent, lay it down, and surrender it to His lordship today.
2.  **Find a Discipler:** Discipleship cannot happen in a vacuum. You need an older, more mature believer in your life who has permission to ask you hard questions and correct your behavior. If you do not have one, ask your pastor to help you find one.
